The content of scientific books:

A science book is a book of non-fiction, often written by a scientist, researcher, or professor such as Stephen Hawking ( A Brief History of Time ), or sometimes by non-scientists such as Bill Bryson (A Short History of Almost Everything). These books are usually written for a wide audience. He is supposed to have a general education rather than specific scientific training, as opposed to a very narrow audience who would have access to a scientific paper, and thus be referred to as popular science. Also, it would require great talent on the part of the author to explain subjects difficult enough to people who are completely new to this. subject matter, and a good amalgamation of storytelling and technical writing. In the United Kingdom, the Royal Society Awards for Science Books are among the most prestigious awards for science writing. In the United StatesThe National Book Awards were briefly a category for science writing in the 1960's, but now they have broad categories of fiction and nonfiction. There are many disciplines that are well explained to teach people through science books. Some examples include Carl Sagan on astronomy, Jared Diamond on geography, Stephen Jay Gould and Richard Dawkins on evolutionary biology, David Eagleman on neuroscience, Donald Norman on usability and cognitive psychology, Steven Pinker , Noam Chomsky , and Robert Ernstein on linguistics and cognitive science Donald Johansson and Robert Adrey on paleoanthropology, Desmond Morris on zoology and anthropology, and Fulvio Melia on black holes.
